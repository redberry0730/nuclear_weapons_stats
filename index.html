The Most Dangerous Weapon Ever Invented:

Why Reducing the Risk of Nuclear War Should Be a Serious Concern

Nuclear weapons are the most dangerous weapons on earth. No weapon is more destructive and there is no way to know and control how far radioactive fallout will disseminate or how temporary these effects will be.


Destruction and Death: Nuclear weapons can destroy a whole city, kill millions of people, and jeopardize the natural environment and future generations through its long-term catastrophic effects.

Nuclear fallout: Tens of thousands would suffer terrible injuries and slowly die from radiation exposure. Residual radioactive material propelled into the upper atmosphere and expanded over major areas of the world from where it fell down. The amount and spread of fallout correlates to the size of the weapon and the altitude at which it is detonated. Fallout may get carried along with the products of a pyrocumulus cloud and fall as black rain within the hour.

Nuclear Winter/famine: The earth’s ecosystem and reduced global temperatures can lead to food shortage all over the world. Cities will burn at an intense rate and create a firestorm, which fans the flames and causes extreme heat. Immense smoke and soot will rise way up into the stratosphere and spread across the globe, blocking sunlight. Since it is at a great height, it will remain there for many years to come, making the whole sky dark and handicapping our agriculture, ecosystem, and temperature.

The possibilities of global devastation and mass extinction are what makes Nuclear war so alarming. A nuclear war cannot be won because it can lead to self-destruction.

Our Approach to Analyzing and Visualizing the Datasets

When choosing a dataset to analyze, we wanted to pick something that is beneficial for society or provide insights about perplexing trends that are hard to see with the naked eye. After looking at multiple datasets, we agreed on the topic of Nuclear Weapons because Nuclear weapons are the most dangerous weapons and the consequences of a nuclear war are detrimental to humanity and Earth.

Our purpose is to simplify the dataset by focusing on certain factors to identify patterns that may help describe, explain, and predict. Through this project, we want to inform everyone about potential efforts to reduce the risks of a nuclear war. We will be taking a look at how the number of nuclear warheads change over time and concrete measures taken by each country in helping to reduce the risk of a nuclear war from breaking out.

Before making any visualizations with the data, we cleaned up the datasets by taking into account any missing values or unnecessary data. We removed certain columns that had much to do with our questions or hypotheses.

We chose to use a variety of graphs, each to their advantage in the type of data we want to show. We implemented a horizontal bar chart, stacked bar chart, choropleth map, and stacked area chart. We chose to use a horizontal bar chart because the data is categorical and to show frequency distribution. Bar chart is useful to compare different categorical or discrete variables. Stacked chart bar charts are best used when showing comparisons between categories, which is suitable for comparing countries with the number of nuclear warheads split into four categories. Furthermore, choropleth maps use color to show how data changes from place to place, which is suitable for visualizing information tied to geography, and to compare and analyze data and trends from across locations/countries. Lastly, stacked-area charts can track not only the total value, but also the breakdown of that total by groups. This is helpful when we want to compare the heights of each segment of the curve according to a particular country and allows us to get a general idea of how each subgroup compares to the other in their contributions to the total number of nuclear warheads.

A Deeper Breakdown

Roughly 90 percent of all nuclear warheads are owned by Russia and the United States, who each have around 4,000 warheads in their military stockpiles. In contrast, the rest of the countries do not see a need for more than a couple hundred nuclear weapons to ensure national security.

To better understand the proliferation of nuclear weapons is to quantify the stockpiles the countries have. An important note is that the stacked area chart shown does not include retired warheads awaiting dismantlement. Thus, the total inventories of nuclear warheads is larger than what is shown in the chart. Since the United States and Russia’s military power are so much more advanced compared to that of the rest of the world, there is a huge gap between the two leading military powers and the other countries. As a result, it might be beneficial to look at the stockpiles of nuclear weapons between Russia and the United States in one chart, and compare the rest of the countries in another chart. For both charts, they illustrate the total number of stockpiled nuclear weapons for the respective countries peaked between 1980 and 1990. Notice the difference between the scales for the y-axis, this shows how much more powerful the US and Russia are. Within the stacked area chart without Russia and the US, it is evident that UK, France, and China have the most total number of nuclear weapons over the years.

Nuclear Inventory Breakdowns

Despite the United States’ effort to dramatically reduce its non-strategic nuclear weapons after the Cold War, the combined inventory of nuclear warheads of the whole world remains at a very high level. The majority of the ownerships falls into nine countries, possessing roughly 12,700 warheads as of early-2022. Russia maintains a large amount and variety of non-strategic nuclear warheads. People are concerned that Russia might intend on using these in conflicts. The US military maintains more reserve strategic warheads.

It is estimated that of the world’s 12,700 nuclear warheads, more than 9,400 are in the military stockpiles for use by missiles, aircraft, ships and submarines. The remaining warheads have been retired but are still intact and waiting for dismantlement. Looking at the stacked bar chart, Of the 12,700 total nuclear warheads, some 3,730 are deployed on missiles or bomber bases, which is a combination of strategic and non-strategic warheads. US, Russian, UK and French warheads are all on high alert, ready for use in emergencies.

Russia has a clear numerical advantage in non-deployed strategic warheads, while the US has a numerical advantage in non-strategic nuclear warheads. To promote reduction of nuclear weapons, the United States and Russia could negotiate an agreement with an aggregate limit covering all their nuclear warheads and could offset reductions in the categories in which the country has advantage in.
